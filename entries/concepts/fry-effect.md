# Fry Effect — Microwave Auditory Phenomenon

**Type:** Concept — Bioelectromagnetics
**Updated:** 2026-04-06

## Overview

The Fry Effect (also Frey Effect), discovered by **Allan H. Frey** in the 1960s, describes the phenomenon where humans can **perceive pulsed radiofrequency/microwave energy as sounds** — typically described as buzzing, chirping, clicking, or screeching.

## Mechanism

Pulsed electromagnetic energy causes rapid thermal expansion of tissue in the cochlea, creating acoustic waves that are perceived as sound. This explains how victims heard "strange noises" without any external sound source.

## Relevance to AHIs

The Fry Effect is the leading candidate to explain the **unusual auditory experiences** reported by AHI victims — perceived loud screeching, chirping, or clicking sounds immediately preceding symptom onset. This effect:

- Occurs without any external acoustic source
- Can be highly directional and room-specific
- Is consistent with the "directional locational specific features" noted by NASEM

## Key Technical Parameters

- **Frequency range:** 200 MHz to at least 3 GHz
- **Perception thresholds:** 
  - 1.3 GHz: 267 mW/cm² (peak)
  - 2.9 GHz: 5,000 mW/cm² (peak)
- **Distance:** Subjects can hear pulses from inches to hundreds of feet
- **Perceived as:** Buzz, clicking, hiss, knocking
- **Internal generation:** Covering ears provides no relief
