# Havana Syndrome — Clinical Symptoms

**Type:** Concept — Medical Presentation
**Updated:** 2026-04-06

## Overview

The clinical presentation of Anomalous Health Incidents spans a wide range of neurological, sensory, and cognitive symptoms with acute onset and chronic persistence.

## Acute Symptoms (Immediate Onset)

- Intense head pressure or vibration
- Dizziness and vertigo
- Nausea
- Blurred vision and light sensitivity
- Tinnitus (ringing in ears)
- Ear pain
- Perceived loud screeching/chirping/clicking sounds
- Disorientation and spatial/perceptual abnormalities

## Chronic Symptoms (Long-Term)

- Cognitive impairment ("brain fog")
- Diminished short, intermediate, and long-term memory
- Balance and coordination problems
- Persistent headaches
- Impaired fine and gross motor abilities
- Neurocognitive abnormalities (vigilance, task focus)
- Insomnia
- Fatigue
- Post-traumatic stress
- Depression

## NASEM Assessment (2020)

> "The committee found a constellation of acute clinical signs and symptoms with directional locational specific features that was distinctive to its knowledge. This constellation of clinical features is unlike any disorder in neurological or general medical literature."

## NIH Findings (2024)

- No significant differences in brain structure or biomarkers vs controls
- Higher rates of imbalance, fatigue, PTSD, and depression confirmed
- Severe symptoms acknowledged despite lack of detectable brain injury

## Key Characteristic

The **directional and locational specificity** — symptoms are specific to a particular room, direction, or location — is what makes AHIs unusual and suggests an external environmental cause rather than internal pathology.
