# CDC Final Report — Cuba Unexplained Events Investigation

**Source:** Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)
**Title:** "CUBA Unexplained Events Investigation — Final Report: Havana, Cuba, August 2016 to March 2019"
**Date:** Completed December 3, 2019; released via FOIA January 2021
**Type:** Federal Public Health Investigation
**Ingested:** 2026-04-13

## Overview

The CDC conducted a two-year epidemiologic investigation into mysterious medical incidents involving U.S. personnel in Cuba. Despite reviewing **95 individual cases**, the CDC could not identify a cause, mechanism, or effective treatment.

> "The evaluations conducted thus far have not identified a mechanism of injury, process of exposure, effective treatment, or mitigating factor for the unexplained cluster of symptoms experienced by those stationed in Havana, Cuba." — **CDC Final Report**

## Case Breakdown (95 Total Individuals)

| Category | Count | Description |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| **Presumptive Cases** | 15 | Met the specific epidemiologic case definition |
| **Possible Cases** | 31 | Symptoms were present but did not fully meet the definition |
| **Ruled Out** | 49 | Did not meet criteria for "Havana Syndrome" |

## Timeline of CDC Involvement

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| **December 2016** | First individual (reportedly CIA officer) sought medical evaluation for vestibular disturbances, cognitive deficits, and auditory symptoms |
| **February 2017** | Second official reported similar symptoms; State Dept advisory issued |
| **December 2017** | State Department formally requested CDC inquiry |
| **November 2018** | State Department finally provided **unredacted medical records** to CDC — **11-month delay** after initial request |
| **December 2019** | CDC completed final "For Official Use Only" report |
| **January 2021** | Report released via FOIA to National Security Archive |

## Key Findings

### Inability to Identify Cause
The CDC report was **completely inconclusive** on the cause. The committee did not find evidence supporting any specific mechanism:
- No evidence of sonic devices
- No evidence of chemical exposure at harmful levels
- No evidence of infectious disease cluster
- No evidence of mass psychogenic illness

### The HABIT Protocol
The State Department developed a specific clinical screening tool — the **Havana Acquired Brain Injury Tool (HABIT)** — to assess neuro-cognitive symptoms. The CDC evaluated this tool but found it insufficient for determining causation.

### Competing Theories Evaluated

| Theory | CDC Assessment |
|--------|---------------|
| Directed energy / sonic attack | No evidence found |
| Chemical exposure (pesticides) | No evidence at harmful levels |
| Infectious disease (Zika) | No cluster pattern |
| Mass psychogenic illness | Cannot explain acute symptoms |
| Unknown mechanism | **Only remaining conclusion** |

## Systemic Issues Identified

### Data Access Problems
- **11-month delay** before State Department provided unredacted records to CDC
- State lawyers initially blocked CDC from accessing medical data citing privacy concerns
- CDC did not begin its investigation until **one year** after the first event

### Political Controversy
- The report was held as **"For Official Use Only"** for over a year
- Six U.S. Senators (Shaheen, Leahy, Collins) demanded transparency
- National Security Archive and Senator Shaheen criticized the administration for withholding information
- The Trump administration reportedly withheld the NASEM study from Congress until pressured in late 2020

> "One challenge with this investigation is the lack of a well-defined diagnosis and an uncertain source of exposure attributable to the physical symptoms experienced by USG employees..." — **CDC Final Report**

## Symptoms Identified by CDC

Personnel reported a cluster including:
- Headaches and nausea
- Tinnitus (ringing in ears)
- Disorientation and cognitive deficits
- Vestibular disturbances and vision changes
- Sleep impairment
