## Overview

This page compiles the best available data on the number of Havana Syndrome / Anomalous Health Incident (AHI) victims, broken down by agency, location, and compensation statistics. **Important caveat:** No single definitive, publicly available master dataset exists. Figures vary by source, date, and the criteria used (reported incidents vs. confirmed cases vs. treatment-qualified individuals).

## Total Reported Cases

| Source | Figure | Date | Notes |
|--------|--------|------|-------|
| **GAO Report (to Congress)** | **334** Americans qualified for DoD treatment | c. July 2024 | Includes 15 children; the most authoritative single figure available |
| **FPRI Analysis (Lewis Regenstein)** | ~**1,500** U.S. officials globally have "reported" Havana Syndrome | April 2024 | Likely includes all incident reports, not just confirmed/qualified cases |
| **NBC News** | **~200** Americans reported possible symptoms | Sept 2021 | Early figure, predates many subsequent reports |
| **Heritage Foundation** | "As many as **200** Americans" | Nov 2021 | Based on State Department and intelligence community figures at the time |
| **ODNI Assessment** | **~1,000** cases investigated | Feb 2023 | Total cases examined across 7 intelligence agencies; most attributed to non-hostile causes |
| **NBC News / ODNI official** | Roughly **24** cases could not be ruled out for foreign involvement | Feb 2022 | The subset identified in the CIA interim report as potentially linked to a foreign adversary |

### Understanding the Numbers

- The **334 figure** (GAO, 2024) represents Americans who qualified for treatment in specialized military health facilities — the most concrete, verifiable number available.
- The **~1,000 figure** (ODNI, 2023) represents the total universe of incident reports investigated, not all of which meet criteria for confirmed/qualified cases.
- The **~200 figure** (2021) was an early estimate that predates many subsequent reports.
- The **~1,500 figure** (FPRI, 2024) likely includes broader definitions, potentially counting any person who reported symptoms consistent with AHI.

## Compensation Statistics (HAVANA Act of 2021)

### Program Overview

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Legislation** | Helping American Victims Afflicted by Neurological Attacks (HAVANA) Act, Public Law 117-46 |
| **Signed** | October 8, 2021 |
| **Maximum lump-sum payment** | Up to **$187,300** (one year's full salary for higher-ranking employees), untaxed |
| **Eligibility** | Current and former federal employees and dependents with qualifying brain injuries from AHIs occurring on or after January 1, 2016 |
| **Covering agencies** | CIA, State Department, DoD, FBI, and other federal agencies |

### Approval Rates

| Agency | Approval Rate | Source |
|--------|--------------|--------|
| **CIA** | **~21%** of applicants approved | Senate Intelligence Committee (Dec 2024) |
| **Other federal agencies** | **~67%** of applicants approved | Senate Intelligence Committee (Dec 2024) |

The Senate Intelligence Committee found that the CIA's approval rate was dramatically lower than other agencies, indicating "systemic issues" in how CIA processed claims. The report found the CIA "consistently challenged employees' efforts to receive workers' compensation" and "had not always turned over all documents needed."

### Implementation Timeline

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| **August 2022** | CIA begins HAVANA Act payments to approved applicants |
| **April 2024** | GAO report finds victims confused by the process; inconsistent agency support |
| **July 2025** | DoD begins reviewing HAVANA Act applications for military personnel |
| **Oct 2024** | DoD publishes implementing regulations (Federal Register Vol. 89, Oct 3, 2024) |
| **Ongoing** | DOJ, Commerce, and other agencies have published implementing regulations |

### Total Payments

The exact total amount disbursed under the HAVANA Act is not publicly reported as a single figure. However:

- The wiki's index page notes "Over **$50M** has been paid to affected personnel."
- At up to $187,300 per person, this suggests approximately **250-300** individuals have received payments (assuming maximum payout) or a larger number at reduced payouts.
- **21% CIA approval rate** vs. **67% at other agencies** suggests significant disparity in treatment.

## Canadian Victims

| Metric | Figure | Source |
|--------|--------|--------|
| **Diplomats evaluated** | ~**25** Canadian staff and dependents evaluated by Dalhousie University | Global Affairs Canada |
| **With mild brain injury symptoms** | Approximately **half (~12-13)** | Dalhousie/NSHA MOA findings |
| **Lawsuit plaintiffs** | **17** Canadian plaintiffs in lawsuit against Canadian government | Global News |
| **Total estimated affected** | Estimated at **25-30+** including family members | Multiple sources |

### Key Canadian Data Points

- Symptoms developed mostly in the first 6 months of assignment (2017-2019)
- Canada reduced diplomatic footprint by 50%, reclassified Cuba as "unaccompanied" posting
- Hardship level increased from Level III to V
- RCMP **Project Spectre** closed in 2023 after finding no evidence of criminality
- **Paul Miller** represents 17 plaintiffs accusing Canada of downplaying and dismissing claims
- August 2024 GAC report conclusion: incidents "were **not** the result of a malicious act by a foreign actor" — met with victim outrage

## Data Limitations and Caveats

1. **No official master count:** The U.S. government has never published a single, authoritative total count of AHI victims.
2. **Classification barriers:** Most AHI data is classified or sensitive, limiting public access.
3. **Definition variance:** Different agencies use different criteria — some count all incident reports (~1,500), others count only treatment-qualified cases (334).
4. **Evolving figures:** Numbers change as new cases are reported and earlier reports are evaluated.
5. **Agency cooperation:** The State Department stopped publishing monthly AHI briefings in November 2020, reducing transparency.
6. **Disability vs. injury:** GAO (2024) found that Americans affected "struggled to get proper care" and that some "had been denied medical treatment by their agencies and suffered retaliation."
7. **Canadian vs. U.S. divergence:** Canada's 2024 report concluded no foreign actor involvement, directly conflicting with the emerging U.S. congressional consensus.
8. **Dependents and children:** The 15 children among the 334 qualified for treatment highlights that family members are also victims.
