## Overview

This page compiles international, non-English media coverage and official government responses to Havana Syndrome from outside the U.S. Anglosphere. It captures how the phenomenon is perceived, reported, and politically leveraged across different linguistic and geopolitical contexts.

## Cuba: Denial and Counter-Narrative

### Government Position

Cuba has consistently and vehemently denied any involvement in Havana Syndrome since the first reports in 2016. The Cuban government frames the allegations as a political smear campaign designed to justify hostility against Cuba and roll back normalization of U.S.-Cuba relations.

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| **2017–2018** | Cuban Ministry of Foreign Affairs issues repeated denials. Cuba cooperates with FBI and U.S. investigators, allowing access to embassy premises and residences, but finds no evidence of any harmful device or radiation. |
| **2018** | Cuban scientists from the Center for Neuroscience (CENIC) conduct their own acoustic analysis of the recordings released by the State Department. They confirm sounds match the Indies short-tailed cricket (*Anurogryllus celerinictus*). |
| **2024** | Following the 60 Minutes/Der Spiegel/The Insider investigation, Cuban diplomat **Johana Tablada de la Torre** states: "Havana syndrome does not exist... in reality it is 'Washington syndrome.'" The Cuban government characterizes the syndrome as a fabricated pretext. |
| **2024** | Cuban state media (*Granma*, *Juventud Rebelde*, *Prensa Latina*) frame the allegations as part of a broader U.S. campaign of "psychological warfare" against Cuba, echoing coverage of the U.S. embargo. |

### Spanish-Language Media Coverage

| Outlet | Country | Angle/Theme |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **El País (Spanish edition)** | Spain | Balanced investigative coverage; April 2024 article covered the GRU 29155 link investigation but also gave voice to Cuban government denial. |
| **Granma** | Cuba | Consistently frames "Havana syndrome" as a U.S. fabrication. Emphasizes Cuba's cooperation with investigations and the absence of evidence. |
| **Prensa Latina** | Cuba | Cuban state news agency reports on the syndrome primarily through the lens of diplomatic harm to Cuba — lost tourism, stalled normalization, unjustified embassy drawdowns. |
| **BBC Mundo** | UK (Spanish) | Translates BBC English coverage for Latin American audiences. Generally follows the BBC's balanced multi-hypothesis approach. |
| **Telesur** | Venezuela (pan-regional) | Frames the syndrome as an imperialist narrative against Cuba, with coverage sympathetic to the Cuban government's position. |

### The "Washington Syndrome" Counter-Narrative

Cuba's most prominent rhetorical counter-attack has been to reframe the phenomenon as "Washington Syndrome" — suggesting the alleged condition originates in Washington's own intelligence bureaucracy rather than in Cuban territory. This framing appears across Cuban state media and in Latin American outlets aligned with the ALBA diplomatic bloc.

### Government Statements

| Source | Statement | Date |
|--------|-----------|------|
| **Kremlin (Dmitry Peskov)** | "All of them are nothing more than unfounded accusations." — Rejecting the 2024 media investigation linking GRU Unit 29155 to Havana Syndrome. | April 2024 |
| **Russian MFA** | "Der Spiegel articles on 'Havana syndrome' harm Russian-German relations." — Demanding retractions and accusing Western media of "information warfare." | 2022 |
| **TASS (Russian state news)** | "Moscow rejects claims of complicity in Havana Syndrome cases. Russia has nothing to do with this issue." | Multiple |

### Russian Media Coverage

| Outlet | Language | Angle |
|--------|----------|-------|
| **TASS** | Russian | State wire service. Reports exclusively on official Russian denials and frames accusations as anti-Russian propaganda. Minimal coverage of symptoms or victims. |
| **RT (Russia Today)** | English/Russian | Frames Havana Syndrome as a "mystery illness" with skeptical tone. Emphasizes the mass psychogenic illness hypothesis and the cricket explanation. Highlights contradictions between U.S. intelligence assessments. |
| **Sputnik** | Multiple | Similar to RT — focuses on U.S. government incompetence, the lack of physical evidence, and the political motivations behind the allegations. |
| **Kommersant** | Russian | More analytical; covered the 2022 interim CIA report and noted the 24 unexplained cases that were left unresolved. |

## Germany and Der Spiegel

### Der Spiegel's Role

German publication **Der Spiegel** played a central role as a co-investigator of the 2024 joint investigation (alongside 60 Minutes and The Insider). Prior to that, Der Spiegel broke several key stories:

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| **December 2021** | Der Spiegel reports first suspected AHI cases among U.S. Embassy personnel in Berlin, Germany. Sparks German government interest. |
| **March 2024** | Co-publishes yearlong investigation linking AHI to Russian GRU Unit 29155, using travel records, intercepted communications, and the "receipt" for "non-lethal acoustic weapons." |

### German Government Response

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **BND (Federal Intelligence Service)** | Reportedly cooperated with U.S. intelligence on the Berlin cases. No public assessment released. |
| **Auswärtiges Amt (Foreign Ministry)** | Conducted its own investigation into the Berlin embassy incidents. Limited public disclosure. |
| **German Media (general)** | Coverage largely follows Der Spiegel's lead. Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung (FAZ) and Süddeutsche Zeitung provided analytical follow-up pieces on the Russian connection. |
| **Public Reception** | Strong interest given Germany's own Cold War history with surveillance and the Moscow Signal. The Berlin embassy cluster resonated with German audiences familiar with East German (Stasi) surveillance methods. |

## France

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Incidents** | One suspected AHI case reported among U.S. Embassy personnel in Paris (summer 2021), per BBC reporting. |
| **Le Monde** | Published feature in June 2022 (*M le Mag*) exploring "the puzzle of Havana syndrome" — focused on the suffering of North American (U.S. and Canadian) diplomats in Cuba. French-language coverage generally treats the phenomenon as a genuine but unresolved mystery. |
| **FRANCE 24** | Covered the 2024 joint investigation linking GRU Unit 29155 to the syndrome. English-language broadcast; French domestic coverage was more limited. |
| **French Government** | No official assessment or public statement on AHIs. France does not appear to have experienced AHI cases among its own diplomatic personnel, though this has not been formally confirmed or denied. |

## European Union / Euronews

| Outlet | Coverage |
|--------|----------|
| **Euronews** | Published April 2024 article: "'Havana syndrome' possibly linked to Russian intelligence, media report finds." Covered the 60 Minutes/Der Spiegel/The Insider investigation. Multilingual broadcasting (English, French, German, etc.) gives it broad European reach. |
| **EEAS (European External Action Service)** | No formal public assessment. Some individual EU member states (particularly Central and Eastern European members with proximity to Russia) expressed concern about potential targeting of their own personnel. |

## United Kingdom and British Diplomacy

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **UK Government (FCO/FCDO)** | Internal diplomatic cables leaked to *The Guardian* (November 2019) reveal the British embassy in Havana and Foreign Office staff in London "desperately sought to make sense of the unfolding events." British officials shared media reports about crickets and considered the possibility of psychosomatic causes. |
| **UK Parliament** | No dedicated parliamentary inquiry on Havana Syndrome. The topic has been raised in written questions regarding the safety of British diplomatic personnel posted overseas. The UK government has not confirmed any AHI cases among British personnel. |
| **BBC** | Gordon Corera (BBC security correspondent) produced "The Mystery of Havana Syndrome" documentary/feature, providing balanced multi-hypothesis examination. BBC News reported extensively on the 2021 Vienna and European clusters. |
| **The Guardian** | November 2019 FOI disclosure of UK diplomatic cables; April 2024 coverage of the GRU Unit 29155 investigation. |

## Canada: Government Report and Victim Lawsuits

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Canadian Victims** | Approximately **25 Canadian diplomats and staff** were evaluated by Dalhousie University's Brain Repair Centre for potential brain injury linked to symptoms in Havana (2017–2018). About half showed symptoms associated with mild brain injury. |
| **Global Affairs Canada Report (August 2024)** | Concluded the unexplained health incidents "were **not the result of a malicious act by a foreign actor.**" Attributed symptoms to "a combination of pre-existing medical conditions, environmental factors, and conventional illnesses." The report was met with outrage from Canadian victims. |
| **RCMP Project Spectre** | Canadian federal police investigation into potential pulsed RF and acoustic attacks. Installed sensors in staff quarters. Investigation closed in 2023 after finding no evidence of criminality. |
| **Victim Lawsuits** | 17 Canadian plaintiffs filed a lawsuit against the Canadian government, accusing Ottawa of downplaying Havana Syndrome and dismissing their claims. Lawyer **Paul Miller** stated: "I have always believed that it was a foreign adversary." |
| **Canadian Media** | **Global News** provided extensive coverage, particularly victim outrage at the August 2024 report. **CTV News** and **CBC** also covered the issue. Coverage often contrasted Canadian findings with the shifting U.S. narrative. |
| **Dalhousie University Study (2019)** | Published in *JAMA Otolaryngology*; found vestibular and ocular motor abnormalities. Also investigated organophosphate (pesticide) exposure hypothesis, though subsequent Golder Associates assessment found pesticide risks at "acceptable levels." |

## Asia: Guangzhou, Taiwan, and Beyond

### China

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Guangzhou Consulate Cluster (May 2018)** | Multiple Chinese and U.S. staff at the U.S. Consulate in Guangzhou, China reported symptoms similar to those in Havana. CDC deployed investigators. China's government denied any involvement. U.S. reduced consulate staff significantly. |
| **Chinese Government Response** | Denied any involvement. Chinese state media (*Global Times*, *Xinhua*) framed the allegations as part of a pattern of U.S. accusations against China without evidence, linking it to broader geopolitical tensions. |
| **Chinese Media** | Coverage in English-language Chinese media (China Daily, Global Times) was defensive and dismissive, characterizing the claims as a pretext for U.S. pressure. Limited coverage of the human suffering dimension. |

### Taiwan

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **AHI Reports** | U.S. personnel in Taiwan reportedly experienced AHI-like symptoms. Geolocation data allegedly placed members of GRU Unit 29155 in Taiwan around the time of incidents. |
| **Taiwanese Media** | Coverage focused on the potential threat to Taiwan's own security and the involvement of Russian intelligence operations in the Indo-Pacific region. |

### Japan and South Korea

No confirmed AHI cases reported. Limited media coverage, mainly translating U.S. and international reports.

### Australia

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **AHI Reports** | At least one cluster reportedly involved U.S. personnel in Australia. GRU Unit 29155 members allegedly tracked to locations correlating with incident timings. |
| **Australian Government** | No official public assessment. ASIO reportedly cooperated with U.S. intelligence. |
| **Australian Media** | *News.com.au* published coverage in 2024 titled "Terrifying origin of mystery condition," focusing on "Patient Zero" and accusations of a Washington cover-up. |

## UN and International Bodies

No United Nations investigation or formal response to Havana Syndrome. The issue has not been raised at the UN General Assembly or Security Council. The topic appeared in WHO discussions only tangentially, as the WHO has no mandate for investigating suspected weapons attacks on diplomatic personnel.
