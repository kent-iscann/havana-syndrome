## Overview

The JASON advisory group — an elite panel of physicists, engineers, and scientists — conducted its own investigation into Anomalous Health Incidents and reached conclusions that **directly contradict** the NASEM 2020 report. Where NASEM deemed directed RF energy "most plausible," JASON judged microwave/RF causes "**highly unlikely**."

## Key Finding — The Cricket Theory

JASON concluded with **"high confidence"** that the audio recordings made by Havana diplomats were **not electronic in origin**:

> "We believe the recorded sounds are mechanical or biological in origin, rather than electronic. **The most likely source is the Indies short-tailed cricket** (*Anurogryllis celerinictus*)." — **JASON Report, 2018**

The cricket species is endemic to Cuba and its calls match the frequency and pattern of the sounds recorded by embassy personnel.

## Rejection of Directed Energy

### Technical Arguments
- **"No plausible single source of energy (neither radio/microwaves nor sonic) can produce *both* the recorded audio/video signals *and* the reported medical effects."**
- **Electronic stability:** Wi-Fi, cell phones, and other electronics in affected homes functioned normally during incidents — this would not happen under a high-power microwave attack
- The Frey Effect (microwave auditory phenomenon) was acknowledged, but: **"We judge as highly unlikely the notion that pulsed RF mimics acoustic signals in both the brain (via the Frey effect) and in electronics."**

### Alternative Explanations
| Theory | Confidence Level | Description |
|--------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Cricket sounds** | **High** | Indies short-tailed cricket matches recording |
| **Psychogenic / mass psychology** | **Plausible** | Social contagion of symptoms |
| **Adversary deception** | **Possible** | Sounds used as cover to mask "entirely unrelated mode of causing illness" |
| **Mechanical noise** | Low | Concrete vibrating machine with worn bearings |

## Conflict with NASEM

### Critical Failure: JASON Was Not Shared with NASEM
The NASEM panel that produced the 2020 report **was never provided the JASON report** during their assessment. This is a fundamental gap in NASEM's methodology — they evaluated competing hypotheses without considering the most thorough counter-analysis.

### Expert Critique
Cheryl Rofer, a former Los Alamos National Laboratory chemist, stated:
> "The JASON report is a **very thorough scientific analysis, the kind which wasn't done in the National Academies of Sciences report.**"

This critique echoes the broader concern that NASEM's assessment lacked the physics-engineering depth that JASON brought to the question.

## Technical Methodology Differences

| Aspect | JASON | NASEM |
|--------|-------|-------|
| **Physics rigor** | Deep dive into RF propagation, electronics effects | Primarily medical/clinical evaluation |
| **Audio analysis** | Detailed acoustic matching to cricket | Audio recording not central to analysis |
| **Electronic evidence** | Normal Wi-Fi operation = no high-power RF | Not addressed |
| **Access to classified data** | Full access to recording evidence | Restricted by classification barriers |

## Government Response

The Biden-Harris administration explicitly distanced itself from the JASON findings:

> "The 2018 JASON report is **not aligned with the Biden-Harris administration's understanding of AHI** and it has not informed our response." — **Senior Administration Official**

## JASON Group Background

JASON is an independent group of elite scientists that advises the U.S. government on defense and national security matters. Members typically include Nobel laureates and leading academics in physics, engineering, and related fields. The group has been providing classified scientific assessments since 1960 — the same panel that advised on nuclear weapons testing, missile defense, and other critical national security technologies.

## Significance

The JASON report represents the **strongest scientific counterweight** to the directed energy hypothesis. Its declassification in 2022 (via NYT and BuzzFeed FOIA requests) revealed that the U.S. government had a competing scientific assessment from 2018 that was effectively sidelined in favor of NASEM's more sympathetic-to-directed-energy conclusion.
