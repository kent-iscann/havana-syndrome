## Overview

In 2026, multiple reports emerged that the U.S. government acquired a **backpack-sized device** capable of emitting pulsed radiofrequency energy — the technology suspected in Havana Syndrome attacks. This represents the first potential **physical evidence** of a Havana Syndrome-type weapon.

## The Device

### Description
- **Size:** Backpack-sized (portable)
- **Signature:** Silent, does not create heat
- **Technology:** Pulsed radiofrequency energy
- **Programming:** Shapes "a unique, electromagnetic wave that rises and falls abruptly and pulses rapidly"

> "The device is portable, backpack-sized... It is silent and doesn't create heat... The programming shapes a unique, electromagnetic wave that rises and falls abruptly and pulses rapidly." — **60 Minutes, March 2026**

### Acquisition
The device was reportedly obtained from a **Russian criminal network**. Details of the operation that recovered it remain classified. Some reports connect the acquisition to an operation involving **Venezuela**, where the U.S. has been conducting operations against Russian-linked entities.

### Testing
- U.S. government is **testing the device on animals** (ferrets, according to reports)
- The goal is to determine if the device produces the same neurological effects reported by AHI victims
- The U.S. Army funded a **$750,000 grant** to expose ferrets to RF waves to mimic concussive injuries

### Implications
1. **Physical proof:** If confirmed, this is the first time a device matching the Havana Syndrome profile has been in U.S. hands
2. **Portability:** Confirms that such technology can be carried by a single person
3. **Silent operation:** Confirms no acoustic signature — consistent with victim reports
4. **Testing on animals:** Suggests the government is trying to reproduce victim symptoms under controlled conditions

## Norwegian Scientist Parallel Test

In February 2026, a Norwegian researcher built a microwave pulse device and **tested it on himself**, reportedly developing neurological symptoms consistent with Havana Syndrome. This independent verification adds credibility to the pulsed-RF hypothesis.
