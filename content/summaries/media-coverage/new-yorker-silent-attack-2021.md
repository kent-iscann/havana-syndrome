## Overview

A landmark investigative feature by **Adam Entous** in *The New Yorker* (May 31, 2021), widely considered one of the definitive long-form journalistic treatments of the Havana Syndrome phenomenon.

**Source:** The New Yorker  
**Author:** Adam Entous  
**Date:** May 31, 2021  
**Type:** Long-form investigative journalism  
**Ingested:** 2026-04-13

## Key Findings

### The White House Incident (November 2020)
A senior NSC official was struck while walking on the Ellipse near the White House:
> "In a matter of about seven minutes, I went from feeling completely fine to thinking, Oh, something's not right, to being very, very worried and actually thinking I was going to die."

### Medical Evidence
University of Pennsylvania researchers studied 40 patients via MRI:
- No physical skull impact detected
- **White matter volume was smaller** than in a similar group of healthy adults — indicating structural brain damage
- UPenn specialists described the injury as a **"concussion without a concussion"**

### The Leading Suspect
Top officials in both the Trump and Biden administrations suspect the **Russian GRU** is responsible:
- **Method:** Pulsed microwave-radiation devices aimed at U.S. personnel
- **Purpose:** Likely aimed at stealing data from computers or smartphones, with collateral human tissue damage
- **CIA Director William Burns** privately referred to the incidents as **"attacks"**

### Scope of Impact
By May 2021, the phenomenon had expanded from a few dozen Havana cases to **over 130 possible cases** globally:
- ~50 CIA officers affected
- Also State Department, military, and family members
- Locations: Cuba, China, Russia, Colombia, Kyrgyzstan, Uzbekistan, Austria, and Washington D.C.

### Significance
This piece was one of the first major magazine features to comprehensively document the phenomenon, bringing it to a mainstream audience. Entous's reporting gave the issue significant credibility and helped push it into mainstream political discourse.
