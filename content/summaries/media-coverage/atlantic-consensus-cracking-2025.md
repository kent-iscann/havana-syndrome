## Overview

Analysis piece in *The Atlantic* (January 2025) covering the ODNI's shift in assessment and the growing internal divide within the intelligence community on whether a foreign adversary could be responsible for Havana Syndrome.

**Source:** The Atlantic

**Type:** Analysis/News feature

## Key Findings

### The Intelligence Split
- The CIA led a 2023 report ruling out foreign weapons, but a December 2024 update reveals a significant internal divide
- Two intelligence agencies shifted to acknowledge a "greater possibility" of foreign involvement
- One agency now sees **"roughly even" (50/50) chance** a foreign actor used a novel weapon against U.S. personnel
- Another sees **"roughly even chance"** such a weapon exists but doubts it was deployed
- **Five of seven agencies** maintain their original skeptical position
- **The NSA** is reportedly one of the agencies that changed its view, possibly due to intercepted communications about foreign research

### Reconsidering Directed-Energy Technology
- The IC is now reconsidering the plausibility of directed-energy technology
- Intelligence indicates foreign nations' directed energy research programs have been "making progress"
- A subset of cases may involve capabilities not previously recognized

### White House Response
- The Biden Administration took action on the new findings
- The ODNI assessment was updated to reflect the internal disagreement
- The shift signals that the White House is taking the foreign adversary theory more seriously than in previous years

### Significance
The Atlantic's coverage represents mainstream elite media picking up on the internal intelligence community disagreement, lending further credibility to the directed-energy hypothesis. The piece notes that while the majority of agencies remained skeptical, even a partial shift represents a significant crack in what was previously a unified dismissal.
