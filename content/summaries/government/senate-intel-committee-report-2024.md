## Overview

A declassified Senate Intelligence Committee report found that the CIA's handling of Havana Syndrome cases was marked by **flawed medical care**, **delayed compensation**, and **communication failures**, resulting in harm to affected personnel.

## Key Findings

### Care Failures
- Nearly **100 CIA-affiliated individuals** reported AHIs
- Many faced **"delayed, denied or pre-conditioned care"**
- CIA shifted away from supporting affected personnel **more than a year** before its official conclusion that foreign adversaries were unlikely to be responsible
- Medical providers noted **"significant moral injury"** affecting patient recovery

### Compensation Disparity
- **Only 21%** of CIA workers' comp applicants approved **vs 67% at other agencies**
- CIA **contested claims more aggressively** than other departments
- CIA often declined to confirm basic facts about reported incidents

### Unanswered Questions
Despite concluding foreign adversary involvement was "very unlikely," the report warns:
> "Many unanswered questions" due to research gaps
> "US adversaries are likely developing directed energy technologies that may plausibly explain some of the reported symptoms."
> "It can take years or even decades for some medical mysteries to be solved."

### Moral Injury
> "Many AHI reporters experienced a significant moral injury as a result of how they perceived CIA's treatment of them."
> Medical providers observed this affecting patients' recovery due to "increased stress about not being believed."
