## Overview

The FBI conducted two separate investigations into Anomalous Health Incidents: one beginning in **May 2017** for Cuba cases, and one in **May 2018** for China (Guangzhou) cases. The FBI's investigation represents one of the three major federal inquiries alongside the CDC and NASEM.

## FBI Behavioral Analysis Unit (BAU) Findings — 2018

The FBI's Behavioral Analysis Unit assessed the Havana Syndrome incidents as a **"mass psychogenic illness"** — a psychological/social phenomenon rather than a physical attack.

### Key BAU Assessment Points
- The BAU concluded that victims were experiencing **mass psychogenic illness**
- The victims were assessed as "sincerely and honestly" reporting their experiences
- The framing of symptoms "as possible attacks by an unknown mechanism that could cause permanent harm such as brain damage" played an influential role in how symptoms were perceived
- High-stress embassy environment was identified as a contributing factor
- No physical evidence of weapons or attacks was found

### 2024 Report Release
The FBI released a redacted version of the BAU report in August 2024 following a FOIA request by the New York Times. The report confirmed:
- Initial assessment that AHI was "mass psychogenic illness"
- Investigation was hampered by lack of physical evidence
- BAU could not identify any mechanism for physical attacks

## Role in Kovalev Investigation

The FBI's most concrete investigative work came through the **80-hour interview of Vitalii Kovalev** ("The Chef") — a Russian military electrical engineer with top-secret clearance who was arrested in Florida after a high-speed chase in 2020.

### FBI Agent "Carrie"
- FBI Agent "Carrie" interviewed Kovalev for **80 hours**
- Kovalev had a device to erase GPS data in his car
- Notes on bank accounts were found
- Kovalev allegedly identified by a victim in Georgia (Tbilisi) during an AHI incident
- Kovalev returned to Russia and was reportedly killed in Ukraine in 2023

## Controversy and Criticism

### Criticism by Victims
- FBI's "mass psychogenic illness" assessment became a central point of contention
- Victims felt the FBI dismissed their physical symptoms as psychological
- The assessment was used by the intelligence community to support the "very unlikely" foreign adversary conclusion

### NY Times Co. v. FBI Lawsuit
The New York Times filed a lawsuit seeking release of the FBI report. The court noted:
- The FBI failed to sustain its claim that disclosure would interfere with enforcement proceedings
- The BAU's conclusion of "mass psychogenic illness" was revealed through the legal process
