**Type:** Concept — Functional Neurological Disorder
**Updated:** 2026-04-13

## Overview

Persistent Postural-Perceptual Dizziness (PPPD) is a functional (not psychiatric) vestibular disorder that can be triggered by an initial vestibular insult and then persist independently. It was diagnosed in approximately **25% of patients** evaluated by the NIH in the Havana Syndrome cohort.

## NASEM Assessment

The NASEM committee (2020) found that PPPD could explain the **chronic phase** of Havana Syndrome symptoms, particularly:
- Persistent dizziness and imbalance
- Symptoms worsened by upright posture or active/passive motion
- Sensitivity to visual motion (e.g., scrolling screens, busy visual environments)

### Key Insight
> PPPD can be triggered by an **initial vestibular insult** (like an RF attack or other physical event) and then persist as a functional disorder — meaning the **original trigger could be physical** while the **chronic symptoms are maintained through neurological adaptation**.

### Treatment Window
- Multi-disciplinary plan (physical therapy, psychological therapy, medication) over **3–6 months** typically reduces symptoms to non-impaired level
- Patients remain susceptible to temporary symptom exacerbations from provocative stimuli
- **10–20%** of patients who have PPPD for 4+ years remain work-disabled despite maximal treatment

## Relationship to AHIs

- PPPD **does not explain** the acute, directional, location-specific onset symptoms
- PPPD **can explain** the chronic persistence of symptoms after an initial trigger
- The NASEM committee found PPPD to be a **secondary reinforcing mechanism**, not a primary cause
