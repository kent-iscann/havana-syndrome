## Overview

Organophosphates (OPs) are neurotoxic compounds that inhibit acetylcholinesterase (AChE), the enzyme responsible for breaking down the neurotransmitter acetylcholine. Chronic low-dose exposure can cause lasting neurological damage, particularly to the basal forebrain and brainstem nuclei.

## Evidence in AHIs

### Friedman Dalhousie Study (2019) — Supporting
- **26 Canadian diplomats** in Havana showed evidence of acquired neurotoxicity
- **Lower serum AChE and BChE** in recently exposed individuals
- **Temephos** (organophosphate) detected in 60% of remotely exposed subjects
- **Blood-brain barrier leakage** — consistent with OP neurotoxicity
- White matter degradation in specific brain structures
- Aggressive Zika fumigation in Cuba starting January 2017

### NASEM Report (2020) — Skeptical
- Deemed chemical exposure **"unlikely"** as primary cause
- Canadian study criticized for measuring AChE in serum rather than whole blood
- No environmental samples from U.S. residences showed OPs at testing time
- Caveat: OPs degrade quickly — absence at testing time doesn't prove absence at exposure time
- **U.S. clinical history "not consistent with acute OP poisoning"** (cholinergic crisis)

### Key Discrepancy
- The Dalhousie study found strong evidence for OP neurotoxicity
- NASEM dismissed this evidence
- The Canadian cohort **lacked acute directional auditory symptoms** that U.S. personnel reported — suggesting **different causes for different populations**

## Mechanism

- OPs inhibit AChE → acetylcholine accumulates → overstimulation of cholinergic synapses
- Chronic low-dose exposure particularly affects the basal forebrain
- Symptoms match chronic AHIs: headaches, cognitive deficits, fatigue, concentration issues
