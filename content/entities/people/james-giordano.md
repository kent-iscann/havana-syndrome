## Overview

James Giordano, PhD, MPhil, is a neuroscientist and ethicist widely regarded as the most significant figure in the human evaluation side of Havana Syndrome research. He served as lead forensic evaluator for the original cohort of American diplomats affected in Havana in 2016 and has been a persistent advocate for the directed-energy hypothesis throughout the subsequent decade of debate.

## Academic Background

Giordano is Professor in the **Departments of Neurology and Biochemistry**, Chief of the **Neuroethics Studies Program**, Co-Director of the **O'Neill-Pellegrino Program in Brain Science and Global Health Law and Policy**, and Scholar-in-Residence at the **Pellegrino Center for Clinical Bioethics** at Georgetown University Medical Center, Washington, DC.

His international academic appointments include:
- **Distinguished Visiting Professor** of Brain Science, Health Promotions and Ethics at Coburg University of Applied Sciences, Germany
- **JW Fulbright Foundation Visiting Professor** of Neurosciences and Neuroethics at Ludwig-Maximilians University, Munich, Germany (2011-2012)
- Research Fellow and Task Leader for the EU Human Brain Project Sub-Project on Dual-Use Brain Science
- Appointed member of the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services Secretary's Advisory Council on Human Research Protections (SACHRP)
- Senior Scientific Advisor to the Strategic Multilayer Assessment Branch of the Joint Staff of the Pentagon

Giordano is also affiliated with the **Center for Disruptive Technology and Future Warfare** at the **National Defense University's Institute for National Strategic Studies**, where he has published extensively on directed-energy and bioweapons research.

## Cuban Embassy Evaluation — 2017

### The Hotel Nacional Mission

In early 2017, Giordano was sent to Havana to conduct forensic evaluations of the original **17 affected U.S. diplomatic victims** at the Hotel Nacional de Cuba — the site where many of the reported incidents occurred.

He produced a detailed **14-page forensic evaluation report** documenting the clinical presentations of this first cohort. His findings included:
- Documented balance and coordination dysfunction
- Diminished short-, intermediate-, and long-term episodic and procedural memory
- Impairment of fine and gross motor abilities
- Neurocognitive abnormalities affecting vigilance and task focus

### Two Primary Suspects Identified

Giordano's evaluation identified two plausible mechanisms for the symptoms:

**1. Ultrasonic Stimulation** — High probability based on clinical presentation and environmental data. Could be focused directionally and produce effects consistent with victim reports.

**2. Pulsed Microwaves** — High probability, consistent with the [[fry-effect|Fry Effect]] (microwave auditory phenomenon). Known technological capabilities existed by 2016. Could be focused on one room without affecting adjacent rooms.

Giordano wrote: *It was — and remains — unclear whether exposure to an ultrasonic and/or microwave stimulus occurred singularly, in some combination, or in tandem.*

### Alignment with NASEM

Giordano's 2017 findings were later corroborated by the 2020 [[nasem-report-2020|NASEM report]] led by [[david-relman|David Relman]]. The committee found a constellation of clinical signs distinctive to their knowledge, unlike any disorder in neurological or general medical literature.

## Key Publications

### National Defense Magazine — April 2024

Published as a featured expert analysis in *National Defense Magazine*, titled "Directed Energy Remains Key Suspect Behind Havana Syndrome." Key arguments:
- Technologies required were extant, scalable, fieldable, and in operational use by 2016
- Evidence of R&D by both transatlantic and transpacific near-peer competitors
- AHIs represent a clear and present risk requiring shifts in political, intelligence, and military postures
- Criticized the 2023 ODNI assessment for aggregating all cases without distinguishing verified Havana cases from later unverified reports

### NDU Research Papers

- **Giordano & DiEuliis (2022)** — "Anomalous Health Incidents of the Havana Syndrome: Implications and Lessons for Global Biosecurity and Defense" (NDU Center for the Study of Weapons of Mass Destruction). Argued the original two dozen Havana cases are verified physical injuries and that multinational state-of-the-science is sufficiently advanced to produce such devices.

- **Giordano (2024/2025)** — "Evaluating Anomalous Health Incidents Of The Havana Syndrome: The Case For A Structured Qualitative And Quantitative Symptom Assessment Instrument" — Published via NDU Digital Commons and EC Neurology, calling for systematic symptom assessment protocols.

## Congressional Testimony and Advocacy

Giordano has testified before Congress on multiple occasions regarding Havana Syndrome, providing his forensic findings and advocating for continued investigation into directed-energy weapon causation. He has been a persistent voice challenging the U.S. Intelligence Community's dismissive assessments, particularly the 2023 ODNI report that concluded foreign involvement was "very unlikely."

His criticism centered on methodological flaws: the intelligence community's failure to distinguish the original verified Havana cases from the much larger pool of unverified self-reports that followed once the syndrome became widely known.

## Significance

Giordano occupies a unique position in the Havana Syndrome narrative: he was among the very first scientists to examine the original victims firsthand, produced the foundational forensic documentation, and has remained a consistent voice advocating for the directed-energy hypothesis through the decade of institutional debate, denial, and eventual partial vindication that followed.

His work bridges clinical neurology, national defense, bioethics, and forensic evaluation — making him arguably the most significant individual figure in the scientific investigation of Havana Syndrome.
