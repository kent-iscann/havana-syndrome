## Overview

Allan H. Frey is the neuroscientist who discovered the **microwave auditory effect** in 1961 — the phenomenon now central to the directed energy hypothesis for Havana Syndrome. The effect is commonly known as the **"Frey Effect."**

## Discovery and Research

### The Microwave Auditory Effect (1961)
Frey discovered that humans can perceive **pulsed or modulated radio frequencies as audible sounds** (clicks, buzzing, hissing, knocking) generated directly inside the head, without any external acoustic source.

> "The induced sounds were described as 'a buzz, clicking, hiss, or knocking, depending on several transmitter parameters, i.e., pulse width and pulse-repetition rate.'" — **Allan H. Frey**

### Key Technical Findings
- **Frequency range:** 200 MHz to at least 3 GHz
- **Loudness:** Linked to **peak power density** rather than average power density
- **Thresholds:** 
  - 1.3 GHz: 267 mW/cm² (peak)
  - 2.9 GHz: 5,000 mW/cm² (peak)
- **Distance:** Subjects could hear pulses from inches to hundreds of feet from transmitter
- **Physical sensations:** Beyond sound, certain parameters induce "pins and needles" or "severe buffeting of the head"
- **Nerve deafness experiments:** Tested with nerve-deaf subjects to determine if cochlea was primary detection mechanism

## Relevance to Havana Syndrome

The Frey Effect is the **primary mechanism proposed by NASEM** (2020) to explain the "strange sounds" reported by Havana Syndrome victims. Key points of consistency:

1. Victims described clicking, chirping, buzzing — matching Frey's documented perceptions
2. Effects are directionally specific and room-specific — consistent with focused RF beam
3. Covering ears provides no relief — sound generated internally, not externally
4. Effects can occur without damage to surrounding electronics (at certain frequencies)

## Historical Context

### Cold War Research
Frey's work was conducted during the height of the Cold War, when the Soviet Union was actively irradiating the U.S. Embassy in Moscow with microwaves (the "Moscow Signal"). His research raised concerns about potential weaponization of microwave technology.

### U.S. Military Interest
The U.S. military subsequently funded several classified projects exploring microwave auditory applications:
- **Project "Hello"** — Creating disturbing sounds in people's heads
- **Project "Goodbye"** — Testing microwaves for crowd control  
- **Project "Goodnight"** — Exploring lethal microwave weapons

### MEDUSA Project (2003-2004)
The U.S. Navy contracted WaveBand Corp to design the "Mob Excess Deterrent Using Silent Audio" — a system to temporarily incapacitate personnel using the microwave auditory effect.

## Academic Impact

Frey's 1962 paper became foundational in the field of bioelectromagnetics. His work has been cited extensively in discussions of:
- Havana Syndrome as directed energy exposure
- Potential weaponization of electromagnetic effects
- Safety standards for RF exposure
- V2K (Voice-to-Skull) conspiracy theories

## Legacy and Criticism

While Frey's discovery of the basic phenomenon is **universally accepted**, the question of whether it could cause the **permanent neurological damage** reported by Havana Syndrome victims remains debated. The 2002 PMC article "Can the Microwave Auditory Effect Be 'Weaponized'?" notes:

- **Feasible for** disturbing sounds: Yes
- **Feasible for** serious brain injury at the required power levels: Questionable
- **Feasible for** stealth attacks: Requires equipment that is generally too large
