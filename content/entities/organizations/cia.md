## Overview

The CIA has been central to both the investigation of Anomalous Health Incidents and the controversy surrounding the government's response to affected personnel.

## Role in AHI Events

### Havana Station Withdrawal (2017)
- The CIA **closed its Havana station** and withdrew all personnel in September 2017
- Two CIA "temporary duty" (TDY) intelligence officers fell ill at the [[hotel-nacional-havana|Hotel Nacional Havana — Site of August 2017 Incident]] on August 22, 2017
- The ARB confirmed these injuries matched the pattern of earlier State Department cases
- The ARB criticized the CIA for **"excessive secrecy"** that prevented State Department from acting on the information

### Investigations and Assessments
- CIA Director William Burns reportedly **warned Russian intelligence counterparts in 2021 and 2022** of "consequences" if attacks continued
- **CIA Interim Report (February 2022):** Concluded it was **"unlikely"** a hostile foreign power was responsible for most cases
- The report was highly controversial — victims felt gaslighted and believed it was a cover-up
- Publicly reported incidents appeared to **decline following Burns' warnings**

### Mishandling of AHI Cases (per Senate Report, 2024)
- Only **21%** of CIA workers' comp applicants approved vs **67%** at other agencies
- The CIA **contested claims more aggressively** than other departments
- CIA often **declined to confirm basic facts** about reported incidents
- Nearly 100 CIA-affiliated individuals reported AHIs
- Many faced **"delayed, denied or pre-conditioned care"**
- Medical providers noted **"significant moral injury"** affecting patient recovery

### Compensation Program
- CIA began making compensation payments in August 2022 under the HAVANA Act
- Payments to current and former officers with traumatic brain injuries
